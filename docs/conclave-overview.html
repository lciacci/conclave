Self-hosted multi-model inference lab · Tailscale-only · RunPod / AWS

Three models deliberate.
Then the data says: route, don't judge.

Conclave set out to fan one question across a fleet of open-weight models and let a meta-reasoner pick the best answer. It rigorously measured that idea — and disproved it. Even on an ideal modern fleet, a judge loses to just calling the strongest model. The finding that survived: pick the right model per request, and the real deliverable is the instrument that tells you which. With that settled, the lab turned practical — running real agentic work on the owned model, local-first.

The thesis

The judge was the hypothesis. Routing is the answer.

Arbiter, judge, mediator, critic, router — all meta-reasoners over specialized outputs; the exploration is which one a fleet actually rewards. Conclave measured it. On the ideal modern fleet (Qwen3-32B / Gemma-3-27B / Mistral-3.2-24B, comparable strength, three lineages) headroom is just +0.040, and an in-fleet judge scores 0.920 — below the strongest single model at 0.935. A judge picks after generating N answers, for N× the cost and a saturated gap; a router picks from the query alone, at 1/N the cost — and pairwise grading shows the per-query winner genuinely splits across all three models, so a router has a real signal. The reusable output isn't the judge; it's divergence.py, which measures whether any fleet is worth ensembling for $0, before you build.

  • pluggableThe judge target is a config value — model name + URL — not hardcoded. Swapping in-fleet ↔ frontier is a one-line change.
  • defaultIn-fleet Gemma-9B. Its lineage is decorrelated from the two Qwen candidates, so it's less likely to share their blind spots. No frontier model in the critical path.
  • evalFrontier is a baseline to beat, never the default. The identical ensemble was run with judge=gemma vs judge=frontier and graded blind against gold references. That comparison is the experiment — result below.

The precondition — does this fleet have headroom?

Fan-out + judge only pays if the candidates are comparably strong and genuinely disagree. That's a property of the fleet, measurable before you build any judge. The metric: headroom = a perfect judge − the best single model. Ours is +0.028.

Even a perfect judge beats the best single model by only +0.028 here — so no judge, however good, has much to win. The real judge does worse than that: 0.050 below simply always calling the coder model, at 3× the inference plus a judge call plus the ~30% contention tax.

⚠️ But that first measurement was SATURATED, so it settled nothing. On 31 of 36 queries the top candidate already scored full marks, where headroom is zero by construction. The verdict wasn't negative — it was undecidable. So we wrote 30 harder queries and froze them before any model answered (pre-registered, so they couldn't be tuned toward a flattering result) and measured again.

The hard queries did their job — the ceiling collapsed, scores fell from 0.933 to 0.696, ties fell from 78% to 40%, and the models now visibly disagree on 80% of queries. And the headroom did not move. The ceiling was hiding nothing. (An earlier claim that the verdict was now statistically resolved is RETRACTED — it used a z-quantile with an estimated sigma; with the correct t-quantile the decision threshold falls inside the confidence interval on both query sets. Nothing is resolved.)

Why disagreement tripled but the ensemble's value didn't — divergence is NOT headroom. The fleet is hierarchical, not complementary: the models argue constantly, but when they argue, the coder is usually the one that's right. The killer diagnostic: the coder wins all three categories — it beats the reasoner at reasoning and the general model at general, on a balanced query set. The fleet has no specialists. At this scale, parameter count beats specialization: a 14B model is just better than a 7B and a 9B, even on their home turf.

But the pattern DOES pay — from sampling, not from a fleet

The oracle bounds selection — a judge picking among candidates, or a router. It does not bound generation. So we dropped the two weaker models and sampled the best model 8× instead. Same queries, same judge, same grader. The only change: where the candidates come from.

🔴 RETRACTED (code review, 2026-07-14). The Self-MoA judge gain (+0.058) is VOID: the baseline was graded with 3 grader samples while the Self-MoA arms used 1 (a single noisy grade compared against a mean-of-three), and the judge was the same model as the grader — it graded the answer it had chosen. On a matched baseline the gain is +0.047 with a CI that crosses zero. The ceiling comparison (ORACLE@8 0.813 vs the fleet's 0.722) is confounded by sample count: at matched N and matched grading the real effect is +0.034, not +0.091. The direction survives — sampling one model does raise the ceiling above the fleet's — but the gain is UNMEASURED, not positive. A defensible number needs a judge that is not the grader.

The fleet was never the point. The candidate set size was. The pattern works — it just doesn't work with a fleet of weaker specialists. It works with repeated samples of your best model. And that's cheaper than the fleet design: one model, n=8 in a single request (the prompt is processed once, then forks 8 continuations sharing the KV cache), plus a selector. No co-residency, no contention tax, no fleet to decorrelate.

The durable output is the instrument, not the judge: measure headroom, ties, ceiling saturation, whether each specialist even wins its own category, and whether the verdict is resolved at all — for any fleet, offline, for $0, before building a judge for it.

The judge-vs-judge number, for completeness. Gemma scores 0.883 to the frontier's 1.000 (gap 0.117, 95% CI [0.045, 0.188], p≈0.003) — but see the caveat below: the frontier judge isn't really judging, so this comparison is on shakier ground than it looks.

⚠️ The honest caveat, found by adversarial review. The frontier judge ignores the candidates and writes its own answer on 34 of 36 queries (chosen = −1). So its perfect 1.00 is largely "a frontier model answers an easy question that has a gold reference" — not judging skill. It is pinned at the metric maximum, which means Gemma cannot win: "0 wins" is arithmetic, not a finding. The fix is to force both judges to select among the candidates rather than answer — already supported in the orchestrator, no extra cost.

What the rigor pass got wrong. An earlier 18-query run showed 15/18 ties and read as "the small judge holds up". The rigor pass then over-corrected: its "trap questions discriminate 2.6× better" is not statistically significant (p=0.146), and the traps weren't traps (every specialist answered them correctly). Its "grader self-bias was refuted" is over-claimed — no self-bias was detected, but by an instrument with no resolution at the ceiling. Both the original result and its correction were quoted with more confidence than the data carried.

v3
built · measured

Ensemble + judge — answered

Client-side orchestrator built and verified. Contention baseline captured (+30% → multi-GPU dismissed); the fleet boots clean on additive memory slices. Then the question nobody had asked: does this fleet give a judge anything to arbitrate? First answer: headroom +0.028 — but on a saturated instrument (31/36 at the grader's max), so undecidable, not negative. Thirty pre-registered harder queries settled it: the ceiling collapsed 86% → 20%, disagreement rose to 80%, and headroom did not move (+0.027). The fleet is hierarchical, not complementary — the coder wins all three categories, beating the reasoner at reasoning and the general model at general. Parameter count beats specialization.

A follow-on claim that repeated sampling of the best model made a judge pay (+0.058) was retracted on code review — the judge was the grader and the baselines were mismatched, so the gain is unmeasured. Route, don't judge is what survived.

v3.5
measured

A genuine-specialist fleet — same answer, harder

Maybe the peer fleet converged because its models were too alike. So: a real-specialist fleet on RunPod — an 80B coder, a 32B reasoner, a 70B general, three lineages, one H200 each. It concentrated harder — the 80B coder won every category and 100% of pairwise tie-breaks. Route, don't judge — now confirmed on three fleets. Genuine matched-strength specialists may simply not exist in current open weights, which is itself the result.

v4
in progress

Practical pivot — real work on the owned model, local-first

The thesis is settled; the point now is use. A $0 4-bit Qwen3-Coder-30B on a 64 GB laptop scored 0.900 vs a rented FP8 80B's 0.949 on coding-QA — underpowered (n=30, CI crosses 0), and where the grader could separate them the 80B won 10–2. So local is the daily driver on cost, not measured parity. Then the test the QA benchmark couldn't run: wired into a live coding agent and driven through real agentic tasks. It works — valid tool-calls, real edits — but it's slow (prefill-bound on the harness's huge prompts) and low-fidelity (it confabulated completing half a multi-step task). Verdict: a supervised fallback tier, not an unsupervised peer — escalate to the 80B/frontier for trust and speed. The failure modes are the escalation signal.

Conclave — a learning lab for cloud GPU infrastructure, inference serving, and the "meta-reasoners over specialized outputs" pattern family. Open-weight models, self-hosted, Tailscale-only, ~$100/mo cap. The honest counter-argument stays on the record: OpenRouter is cheaper unless utilization is high — the justification is learning the infra and owning the stack, not saving money.

Positioning — Conclave is the inference substrate in a three-project system with Tessera (routing / governance policy) and pr-arbiter (a union-recall review pattern). Conclave's "judge doesn't pay" null is select-best only; it does not bind pr-arbiter's union-recall review, where fan-out does pay. The map lives in docs/INTEGRATION.md.
